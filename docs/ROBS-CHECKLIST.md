## What ROBS is
Rollover as Business Startup (ROBS) is a specialized structure that allows you to fund a new business using your existing retirement assets without incurring early withdrawal penalties or taxes. It involves establishing a new C Corporation and a new qualified retirement plan (e.g., a 401(k)). You roll over funds from your current qualified plan into the new plan, and that plan then purchases shares of the C Corporation's stock. This is a strictly regulated corporate financing mechanism; it is not an "LLC pyramid" or a way to use pass-through entities to bypass tax laws.

## Before you start (go / no-go)
* **Rollover Magnitude:** Do you have a sufficient rollover amount to justify the ongoing TPA and administrative costs? (Generally, a minimum of $50,000–$100,000 is recommended to ensure the math works).
* **Governance Appetite:** Are you prepared to maintain strict corporate formalities (bylaws, minutes, resolutions) to protect the entity and the plan?
* **Employment Requirement:** If you intend to work for the "Book Digitization Platform," are you prepared to pay yourself "reasonable compensation" via W-2 to satisfy IRS and ERISA scrutiny?

## Questions for your TPA / counsel
1. How do we establish and document the Fair Market Value (FMV) of the stock at the time of the initial purchase?
2. What specific actions would constitute a "prohibited transaction" under ERISA in our specific operational context?
3. How does our business model affect Unrelated Business Taxable Income (UBTI) for the retirement plan?
4. Who is responsible for the timely filing and accuracy of the annual Form 5500?
5. What are the requirements and costs for the mandatory ERISA fidelity bond?
6. Can I review the Summary Plan Description (SPD) and the formal Plan Document before we execute?
7. What is the required cadence for annual stock valuation updates to ensure compliance?
8. If the "Book Digitization Platform" is sold or undergoes a liquidity event, what is the mandatory exit procedure for the plan?
9. How do we manage diversification rules if the plan holds 100% of a single asset (the company stock)?
10. What is the specific protocol for "corrective actions" if a compliance error is identified?
11. How do we document the "reasonableness" of my W-2 compensation to avoid IRS challenges?
12. What are the personal liability implications for me as a plan fiduciary?

## Implementation sequence
* [ ] Incorporate as a C Corporation.
* [ ] Draft Bylaws and initial Corporate Resolutions.
* [ ] Establish the new Qualified Retirement Plan (e.g., 401(k)).
* [ ] Execute the Rollover from your existing qualified plan to the new plan.
* [ ] Plan purchases shares of the C Corporation via a formal Stock Purchase Agreement.
* [ ] Issue stock certificates to the Plan.
* [ ] Set up corporate banking and distinct accounting systems.
* [ ] Establish payroll and process first "reasonable compensation" W-2.

## Ongoing compliance calendar
* **Quarterly:**
    * Review corporate minutes and formalize any major decisions.
    * Reconcile bank statements to ensure zero commingling of funds.
* **Annually:**
    * Perform formal stock valuation (FMV) update.
    * File Form 5500.
    * Conduct annual plan review/audit.
    * Review W-2 compensation for "reasonableness" relative to industry standards.
    * Renew ERISA fidelity bond.

## Red flags
* **Informal Loans:** Any lending of money between the company and the plan, or between the founder and the company.
* **Personal Use:** Using company or plan assets for personal expenses (e.g., personal vehicle, travel, or home office equipment not strictly for business).
* **Valuation Neglect:** Failing to update the stock's Fair Market Value annually.
* **Commingling:** Mixing personal funds with corporate/plan funds in any account.
* **Documentation Gaps:** Skipping board meetings or failing to record formal resolutions for major transactions.
