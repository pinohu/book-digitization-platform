## 1. The Economics of Capital Access

When founders seek capital, they generally encounter three paths. Professional brokers and "funding companies" often charge a **success fee** (typically 5%–10% of the total capital accessed). It is vital to understand that this fee is not "interest"—it is a service fee for **packaging, sequencing, and placement.**

A broker's value proposition is reducing the "rejection risk" by ensuring the application hits the right desk with the right documentation. If you DIY, you save the cash, but you inherit the **execution risk**: the risk of "burning" your credit profile through improper inquiry sequences or being denied due to poor packaging.

### Cost Comparison Matrix

| Feature | Debt (Interest/APR) | Success Fee (Broker) | Equity (Dilution) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Nature of Cost** | Ongoing cost of using capital. | One-time fee for placement. | Permanent loss of ownership. |
| **Impact on Control** | None (as long as you pay). | None. | High (Board seats/voting). |
| **When it's paid** | Over the life of the loan. | At the moment of funding. | At the moment of exit/sale. |
| **DIY Difficulty** | Moderate (requires discipline). | Low (you pay for the ease). | High (requires sophisticated legal). |

**The DIY Path:** To succeed without a broker, you must act as your own underwriter. This means following the "Three Pillars" with extreme discipline to ensure you do not trigger automated denials.

## 2. Pillar A: Fundable People & Business Credit Files

Lenders do not lend to "ideas"; they lend to **profiles**. Underwriting systems are programmed to reward specific data patterns.

### The Personal Profile (The Guarantor)
Even for business-only products, the "strength of the person" often dictates the initial limits.
*   **Payment History:** The single most weighted factor. Even one 30-day delinquency can reset your "score" for months.
*   **Utilization:** High revolving debt on personal cards signals "cash flow stress," which triggers higher interest rates or denials.
*   **Depth of File:** A "thin file" (new credit) is often treated with the same suspicion as a "bad file."
*   **The PG (Personal Guarantee):** Most small-business products require a PG. Your personal creditworthiness is the collateral for the business's character.

### The Business Profile (The Entity)
To move away from personal liability, you must build a "real" separation. Underwriters look for an entity that exists independently of the founder.
*   **The "Real" Test:** Does the entity have its own EIN, business phone number, professional website, and a dedicated business bank account (BBA)?
*   **The Paper Trail:** A business that moves money through a personal account is a "hobby," not a company. Banks require a clean revenue/expense trail within a business-specific ledger.
*   **Time-in-Business (TIB):** Many institutional products have "hard gates" (e.g., 2 years of tax returns).

### Common Denial Triggers
*   **Thin Files:** Not enough history on either the personal or business side.
*   **Inquiry Sprawl:** Applying for five cards in one week (signals desperation).
*   **DTI (Debt-to-Income):** Your personal obligations are too high relative to your stated income.
*   **Entity Mismatch:** The name on the application does not match the Secretary of State filing or the EIN record exactly.

## 4. Pillar C: The Funding Sequence

The biggest mistake founders make is **"Shotgunning"**—applying for everything at once. This destroys your credit score and signals "high risk" to automated systems. Funding is a **sequence**, not a single event.

### The Phase-Based Playbook

#### Phase 0: Stabilization
*   **Goal:** Clean the slate.
*   **Actions:** Pay down personal utilization; ensure the LLC is active and in good standing; open the Business Bank Account (BBA).
*   **Decision Gate:** *If credit score < 680 or high DTI, do not proceed to Phase 1.*

#### Phase 1: Entity Foundation
*   **Goal:** Establish the business "identity."
*   **Actions:** Register with credit bureaus (Dun & Bradstreet); establish "Net-30" vendor accounts (supplies/services that report to business bureaus).
*   **Decision Gate:** *If business credit report is "thin," wait 3–6 months of consistent activity.*

#### Phase 2: Starter Business Products
*   **Goal:** Build business-specific credit lines.
*   **Actions:** Apply for low-limit business credit cards or small revolving lines of credit using the business's own revenue/history.
*   **Decision Gate:** *If denied, analyze the "Adverse Action Notice." Fix the specific reason (e.g., "insufficient time in business") before the next attempt.*

#### Phase 3: Scale (Term/Line Scale)
*   **Goal:** Access significant capital for growth (e.g., scaling the digitization hardware/AI infrastructure).
*   **Actions:** SBA loans, larger commercial lines of credit, or equipment financing.
*   **Decision Gate:** *Requires a "Full Platter" (see Pillar B) and usually 2+ years of business tax returns.*

## 5. Illustrative Scenario (Hypothetical)

*Note: This is a pedagogical example of how sequencing works. It is not a guarantee of any specific outcome or amount.*

**Objective:** A founder seeks **$150,000 in aggregate exposure** to fund AI infrastructure and expansion for the Digitization Platform.

1.  **Month 1–6 (Stabilization):** Founder optimizes personal credit to 740+. Sets up LLC and BBA. No applications are made.
2.  **Month 7–12 (Foundation):** Founder establishes business credit through vendor accounts. Business shows $5k/month in transactional revenue from the $5/book model.
3.  **Month 13 (Starter Phase):** Founder applies for two business credit cards. **Result:** $15,000 and $20,000 limits (Total: $35k).
4.  **Month 18 (Scaling Phase):** With a 12-month history of business bank statements and a completed `THREE-STATEMENT-MODEL.md`, the founder applies for a business Line of Credit. **Result:** $50,000 limit (Total: $85k).
5.  **Month 24 (Institutional Phase):** Using the `SBA-BUSINESS-PLAN.md` and two years of tax returns, the founder applies for an SBA 7(a) term loan for equipment. **Result:** $65,000 (Total: $150k).

**Result:** By following a sequence, the founder accessed $150k without the "shotgun" approach that would have led to immediate denials.
