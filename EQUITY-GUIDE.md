## 1. Stock Options vs. RSUs
Not all "shares" are created equal. Depending on the stage of the company, you may be granted one of two primary types of equity.

### **Stock Options**
An option is not a share of stock; it is the **right to buy** a share of stock at a specific price (the "Strike Price" or "Exercise Price") for a set period.
*   **The Goal:** You want the company's value to rise significantly above your strike price.
*   **The Risk:** If the company value stays below your strike price, the options are "underwater" and have no value.

### **RSUs (Restricted Stock Units)**
An RSU is a **promise** to give you a share of stock for free once certain conditions (usually time) are met.
*   **The Goal:** You want the company to be successful so the shares have a high value when they are delivered to you.
*   **The Risk:** Very low, as long as the company has a value greater than zero.

### **Side-by-Side Comparison**

| Feature | Stock Options | RSUs |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Upfront Cost** | Yes (you must pay the strike price to own) | No (shares are granted at no cost) |
| **Risk Level** | Higher (can expire worthless) | Lower (always have some value) |
| **Tax Timing** | Usually at exercise or sale | At the moment they vest |
| **Employee Preference** | Early-stage (high upside potential) | Later-stage (steady value) |

## 2. Vesting Explained: The "Earning" Period
You don't get all your equity on day one. Vesting is the process of "earning" your ownership over time. We use a standard industry schedule: **4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff.**

### **The Timeline Example**
Imagine you are granted **10,000 Stock Options**.

*   **Months 0–11 (The Cliff):** You are working, but you have "vested" **0 shares**. If you leave before your 1-year anniversary, you walk away with nothing.
*   **Month 12 (The Cliff Hits):** On your 1st anniversary, 25% of your total grant vests instantly. You now own the right to **2,500 shares**.
*   **Months 13–48 (Monthly Vesting):** After the cliff, you vest a small portion every month.
    *   *Calculation:* 7,500 remaining shares ÷ 36 months = **~208 shares per month.**
*   **Month 48:** You are **fully vested** and own the right to all 10,000 shares.

### **Acceleration Clauses**
Sometimes, life happens. Acceleration protects you during major company changes:
*   **Single-Trigger:** If the company is acquired, all your unvested shares vest immediately.
*   **Double-Trigger (Most Common):** If the company is acquired **AND** you are terminated by the new owners, your shares vest immediately. This protects you from being "let go" after a merger.

## 3. Exercise Windows: Turning Options into Shares
"Exercising" is the act of paying your strike price to turn your options into actual stock.

*   **The Standard 90-Day Window:** Traditionally, if you leave a company, you only have 90 days to exercise your options or you lose them. This is a "liquidity crunch" because you might need thousands of dollars to buy your shares right when you no longer have a salary.
*   **Extended Windows:** We aim to provide more flexibility (e.g., 7–10 years) to ensure employees aren't forced to walk away from their hard work due to cash flow issues.
*   **Early Exercise & the 83(b) Election:** Some employees choose to exercise their options *before* they vest. 
    *   **Why?** To start the clock on long-term capital gains and minimize tax.
    *   **CRITICAL:** If you early exercise, you **must** file an 83(b) election with the IRS within **30 days**. If you miss this deadline, the tax consequences can be devastating.

## 4. Tax Implications
Equity is a tax event. Understanding the difference between ISOs and NSOs is vital.

### **ISO (Incentive Stock Options)**
*Designed for employees; highly tax-advantaged.*
*   **At Exercise:** No regular income tax is owed. (Note: You may trigger **AMT**—Alternative Minimum Tax).
*   **At Sale:** If you hold the shares for >2 years from grant and >1 year from exercise, you pay the lower **Long-Term Capital Gains** rate.

### **NSO (Non-Qualified Stock Options)**
*More common for consultants or in certain structures.*
*   **At Exercise:** You pay ordinary income tax on the "spread" (the difference between your strike price and the current value).
*   **At Sale:** You pay capital gains on any growth *after* exercise.

### **RSUs**
*   **At Vesting:** The entire value of the shares is taxed as **ordinary income** immediately. The company usually withholds shares to cover this tax.

### **Tax Comparison Example**
*Scenario: You have an option with a $1 strike price. The current value is $10.*

| Option Type | Taxed at Exercise? | Tax Amount (Estimated) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **ISO** | No (Regular Tax) | $0 (unless AMT applies) |
| **NSO** | Yes | $9 per share $\times$ your tax rate |
| **RSU** | N/A (Vesting is the event) | $10 per share $\times$ your tax rate |

## 5. What is a 409A Valuation?
You might hear investors say, *"The company is worth $100 million!"* but your strike price might only be $0.50. Why the difference?

The **409A Valuation** is an independent appraisal required by the IRS to determine the **Fair Market Value (FMV)** of our *common* stock. 
*   **Investors** buy "Preferred Stock" (which has special rights and higher value).
*   **Employees** receive "Common Stock."
*   **The Benefit:** Because common stock is "lesser" than preferred stock, the 409A valuation is usually much lower. **A lower 409A is good for you**—it means a lower strike price and more room for profit!

## 6. How to Think About Equity Value
**Rule of Thumb: Equity is a bonus, not a substitute for a fair salary.**

Treat equity like a "lottery ticket with asymmetric upside." It could be worth $0, or it could be worth millions.

### **The Framework: Expected Value**
Don't say, *"I have $100k in stock."* Instead, think:
$$\text{Expected Value} = (\text{Probability of Success}) \times (\text{Value at Success})$$

### **Key Concepts to Remember:**
1.  **Dilution:** As we raise more money to scale the platform (to digitize more books!), we issue more shares. Your *percentage* of the company may go down, but the *value* of your shares should go up if the company grows.
2.  **Liquidation Preference:** In a sale, investors get paid back their initial investment *before* employees get anything. We build the company to ensure there is plenty of value left over for everyone.
3.  **Fully-Diluted Share Count:** When discussing ownership, always look at the "fully-diluted" number. This accounts for all options and warrants, giving you a true picture of your slice of the pie.

## 7. Common Employee Questions

**"How much is my equity worth?"**
It is worth $0 today. It becomes worth something when there is a "liquidity event" (an IPO or an Acquisition). Until then, it is a "paper value."

**"Should I exercise my options before I leave?"**
If you have the cash and believe in the company's long-term future, yes. If exercising would cause you financial hardship, you must weigh that against the risk of losing the options.

**"What if the company never goes public?"**
Many successful companies stay private for a long time. We may look at "Secondary Markets" (selling shares to private investors) or "Tender Offers" (the company buying shares back from employees) to provide liquidity.

**"Should I negotiate for more equity or more salary?"**
*   **Choose Salary** if you have immediate bills, debt, or low risk tolerance.
*   **Choose Equity** if you want to build long-term wealth and believe the platform's $5/book model will scale massively.

**"Do I need a financial advisor?"**
If you are planning to exercise options involving more than $10,000 USD, or if you are worried about AMT taxes, **yes**. Professional guidance is an investment in your own wealth.
